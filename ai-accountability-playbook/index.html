A Playbook for Suppliers Who Intend to Win.

Executive Summary

What this playbook covers, and why it matters now.

The public sector's relationship with artificial intelligence is no longer nascent. In 2025 alone, the UK public sector awarded AI-related contracts worth £1.17 billion, a 54% increase in volume on the prior year, bringing the cumulative value of public AI contracts since 2018 to £3.8 billion[25]. Against this backdrop, procurement and compliance teams are now systematically probing how suppliers use AI across three domains. Risk management. People and workforce integrity. Service delivery.

This playbook sets out, with reference to published government policy, regulatory instruments, and documented governance failures, the standard suppliers must meet and exceed if they are to remain competitive in public sector markets. It draws on the UK's Procurement Act 2023[1], Procurement Policy Notes (PPN 017[3], PPN 002[4]), the NHS Digital Technology Assessment Criteria (DTAC)[6], the Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard (ATRS)[5], the EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)[12], and international standards from Australia, Canada, and the OECD[20][21][22].

The central argument. Complacency is now a disqualifying risk. Buyers are trained, their questions are structured, and the regulatory environment has teeth. Suppliers who treat AI governance as a compliance checkbox will lose marks, lose contracts, and in the worst cases become the next institutional cautionary tale.

Part One

The new procurement reality.

1.1 The regulatory stack

Suppliers tendering for public contracts in 2026 operate within a layered regulatory environment that has been fundamentally reshaped since 2023. The Procurement Act 2023, which came into force on 24 February 2025, introduces the most significant reform of UK public procurement law in a generation[1]. It establishes transparency obligations, enhanced due diligence requirements, and a new competitive flexible procedure that allows contracting authorities to design bespoke evaluation processes, including those that probe AI capability and risk[2].

Sitting beneath the Act, Procurement Policy Note 017 (PPN 017), published February 2025, addresses AI use in procurement[3]. It requires contracting authorities to ask suppliers to disclose whether AI tools were used to create their tender responses. Where AI has been used, buyers may conduct site visits, clarification sessions, and presentations to test whether suppliers have genuine capacity and capability, not AI-generated plausibility.

PPN 002, the updated Social Value Model (mandatory from 1 October 2025), mandates a minimum 10% weighting for social value in central government contract evaluations[4]. This weighting encompasses workforce commitments, local employment, skills and apprenticeships. All of which are directly affected by AI-driven workforce decisions. Suppliers whose AI usage displaces rather than develops people will face measurable scoring penalties.

1.3 The Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard (ATRS)

The ATRS requires public sector organisations to proactively publish clear information about the algorithmic tools they deploy and why[5]. As of early 2026, 14 new entries have been added to the public ATRS register, though analysts note this represents only a fraction of known public sector AI deployments[7]. The standard creates a reciprocal expectation. If buyers must be transparent about algorithms, so must their suppliers. Evaluators increasingly use the ATRS as a lens through which to assess whether a supplier's own AI transparency meets the same standard.

1.4 The NHS DTAC

For suppliers operating in health and social care, the NHS Digital Technology Assessment Criteria (DTAC) is the gateway standard[6]. It assesses digital technologies across five domains. Clinical safety. Data protection. Technical assurance. Interoperability. Usability and accessibility. Digital health technologies, including AI-assisted tools, must meet DTAC minimum standards before NHS organisations will complete a procurement, regardless of the procurement route used[8].

Part Two

Risk mitigation. The five credible dimensions.

Public sector buyers have moved well beyond asking whether a supplier "uses AI". Their due diligence now maps onto five distinct risk dimensions. Suppliers must address each with documented evidence, not narrative assurances.

2.1 Transparency and explainability

Buyers require suppliers to explain, in plain language, what AI systems are deployed, what decisions they influence, and what the decision logic is. This is not merely good practice. PPN 017 anticipates that buyers will conduct clarification sessions and site visits where AI is involved in service delivery, mirroring the ATRS requirement for public bodies themselves. A supplier who cannot produce an Algorithmic Impact Assessment or equivalent on request fails this dimension immediately.

  • Maintain an Algorithmic Use Register, a live inventory of all AI tools deployed in delivery.
  • Produce plain-language explainability statements for each system that influences material outcomes.
  • Participate willingly in buyer-initiated due diligence, including site visits and technical presentations.

2.2 Bias testing and assurance

The DWP Universal Credit AI case, in which an internal fairness analysis disclosed in December 2024 under the Freedom of Information Act found "statistically significant referral and outcome disparity" across age, disability, marital status, and nationality[9], illustrates what unvalidated AI deployment looks like at scale. The Public Law Project criticised the DWP's "hurt first, fix later" approach[10]. Suppliers to the public sector cannot adopt the same posture. Bias testing is a minimum, not an aspiration.

  • Conduct pre-deployment bias assessments across all protected characteristics.
  • Establish ongoing quarterly bias monitoring with documented outcomes.
  • Make bias assessment methodology available to buyers on request.

2.3 Data sovereignty and security

PPN 017 prohibits suppliers from using confidential government tender documents or information not in the public domain as training data for AI or large language models (LLMs). This creates a positive obligation on suppliers to have documented data governance policies that govern what enters any AI system during and after contract delivery. Breaches of this principle could, in theory, constitute a disqualifying failure under the Procurement Act 2023.

  • Publish a clear AI Data Governance Policy covering training data sources and exclusions.
  • Implement contractual controls on data use across any AI supply chain.
  • Comply with UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018 for all AI-processed personal data.

2.4 Exit planning and vendor lock-in

Public sector contracts have defined lifespans. AI systems embedded in service delivery create dependency risks that persist beyond contract end. Buyers increasingly require evidence that AI-driven services can be handed over, migrated, or decommissioned without catastrophic service disruption. This includes data portability, model documentation, and transition support provisions.

  • Include an AI Exit Plan in all proposal documentation for contracts of material duration.
  • Maintain documented model cards for all AI systems used in delivery.
  • Make sure data portability provisions are addressed in contract terms.

2.5 Continuous monitoring and incident response

A system that performs within tolerance at go-live may degrade over time as data distributions shift. Buyers, increasingly guided by the ATRS, expect ongoing monitoring commitments, not a one-time validation. Suppliers must demonstrate active performance monitoring with escalation protocols and public-facing transparency where appropriate.

  • Implement real-time or scheduled performance monitoring dashboards for AI systems in delivery.
  • Define incident thresholds, the point at which a buyer is notified, and the response protocol.
  • Commit to periodic third-party audits of AI systems on long-duration contracts.

2.6 The AI Risk Register, a minimum artefact

Buyer expectation. Evaluators now expect suppliers to produce an AI Risk Register on request. This document should identify all AI tools used in service delivery, risk classification (low, medium, high), mitigation in place, owner, review date, bias assessment status, and data governance control. Absence of this document is increasingly treated as an indicator of immature AI governance.

Part Three

People and workforce integrity.

Workforce integrity is the highest-risk blind spot for suppliers in this space. It sits at the intersection of social value scoring (PPN 002), the EU AI Act's high-risk employment provisions, and the broader macro-displacement risk created by AI adoption. Buyers are not only asking what suppliers do with AI, they are asking what AI does to people.

3.1 Social value scoring, the workforce dimension

Under PPN 002's Social Value Model (mandatory from 1 October 2025), in-scope contracting authorities must apply a minimum 10% evaluation weighting to social value[4]. The workforce outcomes within this model require suppliers to make specific, measurable, time-bound commitments on local employment, apprenticeships, supply chain diversity, and workforce development[11]. Suppliers whose AI deployment decisions reduce headcount, outsource roles, or displace workers without a clear reskilling plan will find it materially harder to score well on these criteria.

  • Map every workforce-affecting AI deployment against PPN 002 social value themes before submission.
  • Provide a Workforce Impact Statement for any AI system that changes roles, tasks, or headcount.
  • Commit to funded reskilling pathways where AI displaces existing role functions.

3.2 AI in recruitment and interim staffing, the high-risk classification

The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689), Annex III, classifies AI systems used in recruitment or selection as high-risk[12]. This includes systems that place targeted job advertisements, filter applications, evaluate candidates, or monitor worker performance. Compliance obligations for high-risk systems include transparency to affected individuals, human oversight requirements, bias testing, and registration, with enforcement deadlines from August 2026[13]. Fines for non-compliance can reach €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover[14].

For suppliers providing interim and temporary staffing services to the public sector, this creates clear obligations. Any AI tool used to source, score, or select candidates for public sector placements is a high-risk system under the Act. "AI laundering", the use of AI to make decisions that are subsequently presented as human judgements, is addressed and prohibited under the transparency and explainability provisions.

  • Audit all recruitment technology for high-risk AI classification ahead of the August 2026 enforcement deadline.
  • Implement mandatory human oversight at every AI-assisted selection decision point.
  • Disclose AI use in candidate selection processes to buyers and, where required, to candidates.
  • Make sure interim staffing supply chain partners meet the same AI governance standards.

3.3 Macro displacement and organisational duty

The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report (2025) estimates that 70% of tasks currently performed by the global workforce will be affected by AI within five years. For public sector suppliers, whose contracts often include social value and community impact obligations, passive displacement is not an acceptable position. Buyers are beginning to ask explicit questions about workforce planning in the context of AI adoption, both within the supplier's own organisation and within the services being delivered.

  • Publish an AI and Workforce Policy that addresses displacement risk and mitigation.
  • Include workforce transition planning in all AI implementation proposals.
  • Report annually on the workforce impact of AI deployment across public sector contracts.
Part Four

Governance failures buyers are trained to recognise.

Experienced procurement professionals, and the compliance functions that now support them, are trained on a canon of governance failures. These cases are not historical footnotes. They are the reference points from which evaluation outlines are built. Suppliers must understand them in depth.

4.1 Case 1. Post Office Horizon, the governance failure archetype

Classification, NOT an AI system

Fujitsu Horizon was a conventional IT accounting system. Its lessons apply directly to AI governance by analogy and precedent.

The Post Office Horizon scandal, described as the most widespread miscarriage of justice in UK history, resulted in over 700 wrongful prosecutions of sub-postmasters between 2000 and 2014[15]. The Fujitsu Horizon IT system contained defects that caused phantom financial shortfalls. Post Office Limited (POL) attributed those shortfalls to human error and prosecuted accordingly. The system was trusted absolutely. Its outputs were never questioned. The institution's culture, "the system never lies", created the conditions for catastrophic harm.

The IoD analysis identifies four compounding governance failures[15]. Uncritical trust in technology outputs. Confirmation bias in decision-making. A legal presumption, now under review, that computers do not make mistakes. A board culture that doubled down rather than interrogated anomalies. Academic analysis by Baber, King, Salmon and Jiang (2024) uses accimapping to demonstrate how the system's "black box" nature denied transparency to all stakeholders[16].

The Horizon principle for suppliers. Any AI system that generates outputs used to make decisions affecting citizens, staff, or contract delivery must be interrogatable. Suppliers must demonstrate, not merely assert, how the system reached its outputs. Blind institutional trust in technology is the single most dangerous governance posture in public sector AI deployment.

4.2 Case 2. Dutch SyRI, the first court-halted AI system

AI system, court-halted

The Dutch System Risk Indication (SyRI) was an AI-based fraud detection algorithm used by the Dutch government to identify welfare fraud. In February 2020, The Hague District Court ruled that SyRI violated Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights (right to private life), making it the first judicial decision ever to halt the operation of a government AI system on human rights grounds[17]. The court found that the system operated without sufficient transparency, that affected individuals had no meaningful ability to understand or challenge its logic, and that it disproportionately targeted low-income communities.

Lesson for suppliers. Hidden algorithmic harm will be litigated. The absence of explainability is not merely a compliance gap, it is a justiciable human rights violation. Any supplier deploying AI that influences decisions about individuals must provide meaningful transparency and challenge mechanisms.

4.3 Case 3. DWP Universal Credit AI, the active bias warning

AI system, active in 2024

The DWP's machine-learning system, used to risk-score Universal Credit Advances claims, was the subject of an internal fairness assessment in February 2024. Documents released under the Freedom of Information Act and reported by The Guardian in December 2024 revealed "statistically significant referral and outcome disparity" for age, disability, marital status, and nationality[18]. The DWP stated there were "no immediate concerns of unfair treatment" because final decisions involve a human, but acknowledged that protected characteristics including race, sex, sexual orientation, religion, and gender reassignment had not been assessed for bias[9].

Civil society organisations characterised this as a "hurt first, fix later" approach, a framing that has entered the procurement lexicon[10]. Buyers now ask suppliers explicitly whether they have conducted pre-deployment bias assessments and whether they proactively disclose findings, rather than waiting for FOI disclosure.

The active bias standard. Passive monitoring, testing for bias only after concerns are raised, is now an unacceptable standard for public sector AI. Suppliers must demonstrate proactive bias assessment across all protected characteristics, before deployment and on a regular ongoing basis.

Part Five

Delivery of services. The five standards.

The quality of AI-assisted service delivery is the most direct manifestation of supplier competence. Buyers are moving from evaluating AI capability at bid stage to measuring AI performance throughout the contract lifecycle. The following five standards define the delivery dimension of this playbook.

5.1 Transparent AI use throughout delivery

Suppliers must disclose AI use not just at the point of tendering, but throughout delivery. PPN 017 establishes the principle of ongoing AI transparency, and contracting authorities are increasingly including AI disclosure obligations as active contract management requirements. Any change in AI tool, model version, or deployment scope during delivery should trigger a notification to the contracting authority.

  • Include AI disclosure obligations as standing agenda items in contract review meetings.
  • Notify the buyer within an agreed timeframe of any material change to AI systems in delivery.

5.2 Human oversight at consequential decision points

The EU AI Act's requirement for human oversight of high-risk AI systems reflects a global consensus that consequential decisions, decisions that affect individuals' rights, access to services, employment, or welfare, cannot be fully automated. The DWP case illustrates what happens when this principle is applied defensively ("a human makes the final decision") rather than substantively (human oversight is genuinely meaningful and documented). Suppliers must evidence genuine human oversight, not token sign-off processes.

  • Define, document, and apply human oversight at every AI-assisted decision point affecting individuals.
  • Train staff on meaningful oversight, not rubber-stamping, of AI outputs.

5.3 Performance monitoring and model drift

AI systems can degrade over time as real-world data distributions diverge from training data. This "model drift" is a documented risk in production AI systems. Contracting authorities are beginning to specify monitoring obligations in contract requirements. Suppliers must have active drift detection protocols and demonstrate performance stability.

  • Implement scheduled model performance reviews, at minimum quarterly.
  • Define performance floor thresholds below which human review is automatically triggered.

5.4 Supply chain AI governance

Large contractors increasingly deliver through supply chains that themselves use AI. The principal supplier's AI governance obligations do not end at their own boundary. PPN 017 requires proportionate due diligence, and where sub-contractors use AI in material elements of delivery, buyers will expect evidence that the supplier has applied comparable governance standards down the supply chain.

  • Include AI governance requirements in all sub-contractor agreements.
  • Conduct periodic supply chain AI audits and report findings to the contracting authority.

5.5 Agility in a fast-moving regulatory landscape

The regulatory environment is moving faster than most procurement cycles. The EU AI Act's high-risk system provisions come into force in August 2026, the ATRS continues to expand, and PPN 002's social value model becomes mandatory in October 2025. Suppliers who lock their AI governance into a static document risk falling out of compliance within the contract period. The playbook standard requires a living governance commitment, regular review, board-level ownership, and a clear regulatory horizon-scanning function.

  • Assign board-level accountability for AI governance and regulatory compliance.
  • Conduct biannual regulatory horizon-scanning reviews and update governance approaches accordingly.
  • Communicate regulatory changes proactively to contracting authorities where they affect delivery.

6.1 The global convergence principle

Across all seven jurisdictions, the same four principles appear. Transparency (explain what the system does and why). Accountability (identify who is responsible for outcomes). Fairness (test for and mitigate bias). Human oversight (retain meaningful human control at consequential decision points). These are not optional features. They are the baseline expectation of every sophisticated public sector buyer in the world.

The Australian AI model contract clauses (DTA, 2025) go further, requiring sellers to make sure that AI systems "do not discriminate against any person or group on the grounds of any protected characteristic" and "do not pose a reputational risk or undermine public confidence in the government"[24]. This framing is significant. It positions the supplier's AI governance as a risk-transfer obligation, not merely an internal practice.

Part Seven

The eight complacency traps.

The following are the failure modes where suppliers lose evaluation marks, damage buyer confidence, and in serious cases face disqualification or legal action. Each represents a pattern observed in procurement evaluations, post-award reviews, and the documented case studies in this report.

Trap 1
Static compliance

AI governance documented once, at bid stage, and never revisited. Evaluators now ask for evidence of the last governance review date. A policy with a 2023 timestamp fails the "living commitment" test.

Trap 2
AI laundering in tender responses

Using AI to generate tender responses and presenting them as original analytical work. PPN 017 requires disclosure. Buyers conducting due diligence, site visits, presentations, clarification questions, will expose capability gaps between the AI-written proposal and the human team behind it.

Trap 3
ATRS obstruction

Refusing or delaying buyer requests for algorithmic transparency information, or providing opaque responses that do not meet the ATRS standard. This is increasingly a mark-losing behaviour at evaluation stage and a contract management risk post-award.

Trap 4
Workforce rhetoric without commitment

Social value responses that reference workforce development in general terms, without specific, measurable, time-bound commitments. Under PPN 002 (mandatory from October 2025), vague commitments are scored zero. Buyers are trained to probe for specificity.

Trap 5
AI in recruitment, undisclosed

Using AI-assisted screening, scoring, or ranking tools in the selection of staff, associates, or sub-contractors for public sector placements without disclosure. Under the EU AI Act's high-risk classification (effective August 2026), this is a compliance failure, not merely a transparency gap.

Trap 6
Passive bias monitoring

Testing for bias only after concerns are raised, the "hurt first, fix later" posture identified in the DWP case. Evaluators now ask for evidence of proactive, pre-deployment bias assessment across all protected characteristics.

Trap 7
Supply chain blind spots

Applying rigorous AI governance to the primary supplier's own operations while failing to extend equivalent standards to sub-contractors and supply chain partners who deliver material elements of the contracted service.

Trap 8
Treating AI governance as IT, not governance

Delegating AI governance entirely to technical teams, without board-level ownership, commercial awareness, or integration with bid management and contract delivery. The Horizon scandal's core lesson is that governance failures are board failures. Buyers increasingly ask who, at board level, owns AI risk.

Part Eight

The supplier excellence standard. Five pillars.

The playbook standard is expressed in five pillars. Each pillar defines what "exceeding expectations" looks like in a live procurement evaluation and throughout contract delivery. Together they form an outline for competitive differentiation in AI-governed public sector markets.

Pillar One

Transparent AI governance

  • Maintain a current, board-approved AI Governance Policy.
  • Operate an Algorithmic Use Register, a live inventory of all AI systems in delivery.
  • Produce Algorithmic Impact Assessments for all systems that influence material decisions.
  • Comply with the ATRS standard and be ready to submit documentation on request.
  • Designate a named board-level AI Governance Lead.

Evidence portfolio. AI Governance Policy (dated within 12 months), Algorithmic Use Register, Algorithmic Impact Assessment for at least one live system.

Pillar Two

Bias assurance and fairness

  • Conduct pre-deployment bias assessments across all nine protected characteristics for every AI system used in delivery.
  • Implement quarterly ongoing bias monitoring with documented outcomes.
  • Apply EU AI Act high-risk compliance protocols to all recruitment and employment AI (ahead of the August 2026 deadline).
  • Publish a Bias Assurance Statement as part of standard proposal documentation.

Evidence portfolio. Bias Assessment Report (pre-deployment), monitoring schedule, EU AI Act readiness assessment.

Pillar Three

Workforce integrity

  • Publish a Workforce Impact Statement for all AI deployments affecting roles or tasks.
  • Provide specific, measurable, time-bound social value workforce commitments in all bids (PPN 002 compliant).
  • Fund reskilling pathways where AI displaces existing role functions.
  • Apply AI governance standards, including recruitment AI disclosure, to all supply chain partners.
  • Report annually on the workforce impact of AI across public sector contracts.

Evidence portfolio. Workforce Impact Statement, PPN 002 social value commitments (SMART), supply chain AI policy.

Pillar Four

Delivery integrity

  • Notify contracting authorities of any material change to AI systems in delivery.
  • Implement model performance monitoring with defined drift thresholds.
  • Conduct at least annual third-party AI audits on long-duration contracts.
  • Apply the Horizon principle. Treat every AI output as interrogatable, not infallible.
  • Maintain AI Exit Plans for all contracts of material duration.

Evidence portfolio. Change notification protocol, monitoring dashboard, AI Exit Plan, third-party audit report.

Pillar Five

Regulatory agility

  • Conduct biannual regulatory horizon-scanning reviews.
  • Update AI governance approaches within 60 days of material regulatory change.
  • Proactively communicate regulatory changes to contracting authorities where they affect delivery.
  • Maintain awareness of and readiness for the EU AI Act (Aug 2026), PPN 002 (Oct 2025), and ATRS expansion.

Evidence portfolio. Horizon-scanning log, regulatory update communications to buyers, gap analysis against the EU AI Act.

Conclusions

What separates the suppliers who win.

The convergence of the Procurement Act 2023, PPN 017, PPN 002, the ATRS, the NHS DTAC, and the EU AI Act has created a procurement environment in which AI governance is not optional, not supplementary, and not the sole province of technical teams. It is a core supplier capability, evaluated, scored, and contractually committed to.

The Post Office Horizon scandal, the Dutch SyRI case, and the DWP Universal Credit AI bias disclosure have educated a generation of public sector procurement professionals in what institutional AI complacency looks like and what it costs. These are not obscure references. They are the currency of buyer due diligence conversations.

The suppliers who will win and retain public sector contracts in this environment are those who treat AI governance as a strategic differentiator, not a compliance task. They will produce an AI Risk Register before they are asked. They will conduct bias assessments before deployment, not after harm. They will integrate workforce impact planning into every AI implementation. They will communicate proactively, govern from the board, and build evidence portfolios that can be placed on a procurement table without hesitation.

The competitive advantage argument. In a market where most suppliers are still treating AI governance as a tick-box exercise, the supplier who demonstrates genuine, evidenced, board-owned AI governance capability will score materially higher, win more competitively, and build the kind of buyer trust that underpins long-term contract relationships in the public sector.
